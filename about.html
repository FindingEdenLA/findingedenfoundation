Founder Story

The heart behind Finding Eden

Almost every person I meet eventually opens up about something deeper than they planned to. Stress. Grief. Trauma. Old wounds. Confusion. Loneliness. Sometimes it happens with friends. Sometimes with strangers. Sometimes in quiet moments that somehow turn into honest conversations without warning.

And what I keep seeing is that so many people are carrying more than they know what to do with.

A lot of people do not realize how much of their pain is still living in their body. They do not know how to slow their mind down, sit with themselves, or process what they feel in a way that actually helps. Not everyone connects with clinical therapy. Not everyone can afford it. Not everyone feels comfortable opening up in those environments. Especially men. Especially people who have learned how to function while secretly falling apart.

For me, dance was always soul therapy. It was one of the reasons I came to California. When my first abusive relationship flipped my world upside down and I stopped dancing, I felt how deeply losing that outlet impacted my mental health. It taught me that creativity and movement are not extras. For many people, they are lifelines.

Finding Eden grew from that realization.

I want to create the kind of place people can reach for when they need to breathe, move, create, reflect, and remember they are not alone. A place where someone can take a somatic movement class one night, sit quietly in a calming room the next, join a storytelling circle, make art, write honestly, or simply feel held by the environment instead of pressured by it.

This is both a nonprofit-style initiative and the beginning of a cultural healing movement. A community-centered platform that can host workshops, gatherings, support spaces, and honest creative experiences in Los Angeles — while eventually building toward a more permanent home base.

What matters most is that it feels real. Human. Useful. Warm. Like a place that understands what people are actually carrying.
